Overview

Indigenous Digital Sovereignty refers to the right and capacity of indigenous peoples to control their own digital infrastructure, data governance, and technological development. The movement spans the Americas and Caribbean, representing over 72 million indigenous people and hundreds of sovereign nations.

Central to this movement is the Sovereign Digital Nation framework, which provides a complete alternative to Big Tech platforms through 296 integrated services running on the MameyNode blockchain, secured by post-quantum encryption, and connected via sovereign satellite telecommunications.

"Digital sovereignty is the natural extension of the self-determination our peoples have practiced for millennia. We did not need colonial permission to build civilizations, and we do not need Big Tech's permission to build our digital future." ‚Äî Cacike Soberano

Historical Context

The push for indigenous digital sovereignty emerged from decades of experience with digital colonialism ‚Äî the extraction of indigenous data, knowledge, and labor by technology corporations without consent or compensation. Key catalysts included the documentation of indigenous language data being used to train commercial AI models, surveillance of indigenous activists through social media platforms, and the systematic exclusion of indigenous communities from the digital economy.
